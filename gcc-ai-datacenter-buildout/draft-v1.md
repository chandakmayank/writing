# From Oil Fields to AI Farms: The Gulf's $10 Billion Bet on Datacenters — and the War That Could Stop It

In February 2025, Saudi Arabia signed a $5 billion deal to build a 1.5-gigawatt AI datacenter in the desert. One year later, Iranian drones struck Amazon's facilities in Bahrain and the UAE, shutting down cloud services for millions.

The Gulf Cooperation Council — Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, and Bahrain — had been pouring billions into becoming the world's next AI supercomputing hub. Then the Iran war turned datacenters into military targets.

---

## The Gulf's AI Ambitions

The GCC datacenter market was valued at **$3.48 billion in 2024** and is projected to reach **$9.49 billion by 2030**, growing at a compound annual rate of 18.2%[^1]. But those numbers barely capture the scale of what's underway.

Across the six Gulf states, a combination of sovereign wealth funds, national vision strategies, and partnerships with American hyperscalers is driving what may be the fastest regional AI infrastructure buildout on the planet. Saudi Arabia alone is targeting **3 gigawatts of datacenter capacity** within the next four years — enough to power several million homes[^2].

The strategic logic is straightforward: **oil wealth is finite, but compute demand is exponential**. Every GCC nation has a diversification strategy — Vision 2030 in Saudi Arabia, Vision 2035 in Kuwait — and AI infrastructure sits at the center of all of them.

> The GCC's AI buildout is also a proxy in the US-China technology competition. As the Middle East Institute framed it in a December 2025 report, US firms are using the Gulf as "a force multiplier for US AI capacity that can outscale China's diffusion of its own AI stack through the Digital Silk Road"[^3].

---

## How the GCC AI Stack Works

The strategy, described in detail by the Middle East Institute's "From Crude to Compute" report, operates on a **hybrid model**[^3]:

- **Saudi Arabia and the UAE lead** the initial buildout based on their advanced partnerships with US firms like Microsoft, Amazon, and Qualcomm
- **Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, and Qatar plug in** through coordinated planning rather than watching from the sidelines
- **Sovereign wealth funds provide capital** that private markets can't match at this speed and scale

The Gulf's advantages are real: **abundant capital, cheap solar energy potential, strategic geography** bridging Europe, Asia, and Africa, and — critically — **vast tracts of available land**. Saudi Arabia's HUMAIN has already secured **211 plots of land** across the Kingdom, leveraging the country's geographic diversity for fiber-optic routing[^4].

---

## Country by Country: Who's Building What

### Saudi Arabia: The Scale Leader

Saudi Arabia is building at a pace and scale unmatched in the region.

**HUMAIN**, the state-backed AI company under the Public Investment Fund, is the primary vehicle. Under CEO Tareq Amin, the company has:

- Secured a **$1.2 billion financing framework** from the National Infrastructure Fund, announced at the World Economic Forum in Davos (January 2026)[^5]
- Formed a joint venture with stc subsidiary center3 to develop **1GW of AI datacenter capacity** across the Kingdom[^6]
- Partnered with **Qualcomm** to deploy edge-to-cloud hybrid AI infrastructure[^7]
- Targeted **1.5GW of total national datacenter capacity by 2030**[^2]

Meanwhile, **NEOM's Oxagon industrial zone** is home to an even more ambitious project: DataVolt is investing **$5 billion to build a 1.5GW AI datacenter campus**, marketed as the region's first "net-zero AI factory"[^8]. The first phase — 300MW — is expected to be operational by 2028.

Microsoft is also coming: the company confirmed its **Saudi Arabia East datacenter region will go live in Q4 2026**[^9].

Saudi Arabia's datacenter market is growing at **29% annually** through 2030[^2].

### UAE: The Pioneer

The UAE got there first. **G42**, Abu Dhabi's AI champion, partnered with Microsoft for a **200MW datacenter expansion** in November 2025 — part of a broader **$15.2 billion investment commitment** in the country[^10].

Abu Dhabi also launched **MGX**, a state-owned AI investment firm targeting **$100 billion in assets under management** for AI-driven investments[^11]. MGX serves as the sovereign vehicle for backing AI infrastructure and advanced technology globally.

### Qatar: The Wealthy Entrant

Qatar is moving fast with its sovereign wealth fund leading the charge. The **Qatar Investment Authority partnered with Blue Owl Capital on a $3 billion datacenter venture** in September 2025[^12].

In December 2025, Qatar launched **Qai**, a dedicated entity to "develop, operate and invest in AI infrastructure and systems in Qatar and globally"[^13]. The country currently has **13 operational datacenter facilities with five more in development**[^14].

### Oman: The Connectivity Bridge

Oman's strategy is different — it's positioning as the **digital bridge linking Europe, Asia, and Africa**. Omantel, the national telecom, is transforming into a technology powerhouse with subsidiary **Oman Data Park** serving as the Sultanate's largest datacenter operator[^15].

The company launched a **fibre network with Ciena supporting 1.6 Tbps per wavelength**, engineered for hyperscaler interconnects[^16]. Oman also operates a **120,000 km submarine cable system** through a joint venture with Zain Group, serving clients including Meta, Google, and AWS[^15].

### Kuwait: The Late Mover

Kuwait entered the race in November 2025 when **Ooredoo launched the nation's first AI-enabled data center** in partnership with NVIDIA[^17]. Vision 2035 places AI at the heart of national transformation, with Google Cloud and Microsoft partnerships driving government digitization.

> "Within the next 10 to 12 months, we will see a decisive shift... the opportunity in Kuwait is not incremental, but transformative," a government official told AGBI in February 2026[^18].

### Bahrain: The Cloud Pioneer Under Fire

Bahrain pioneered cloud-first government services with **Amazon Web Services**, becoming home to AWS's Middle East region[^19]. That position of prominence, however, made it a target.

---

## The Iran War: Datacenters as Military Targets

In early March 2026, the US-Israel war on Iran turned the GCC's AI ambitions into collateral damage — or, more precisely, deliberate targets.

**Iranian drone strikes damaged three Amazon Web Services datacenter facilities** — two in the UAE and one in Bahrain — in the first week of the conflict[^20][^21]. Iran's Fars News Agency explicitly cited Amazon's support for "U.S. military and intelligence activities" as justification for the attacks[^22].

The strikes disrupted cloud services across the region and marked what **Fortune called "a new kind of war"** — one where civilian AI infrastructure is treated as a legitimate military objective[^23].

> "Data centers make attractive targets, especially now that the U.S. military is actively using AI," The Motley Fool noted. "The region is likely to see its rapid AI build-out halt completely for the rest of 2026"[^24].

### Can Hyperscalers Walk Away?

Probably not — at least not from what's already built. **The sunk costs are enormous**: power contracts, land agreements, and fiber connectivity that took years to negotiate. Datacenters also need to be **located close to their customers to ensure low latency**[^24].

But **future investment decisions may shift**. Analysts expect hyperscalers to accelerate diversification toward Southeast Asia and India while the Gulf conflict persists. The question is whether the GCC's sovereign wealth funds will double down on resilience — hardened facilities, underground construction, redundancy — or accept a slower buildout timeline.

## What Comes Next

Several developments will determine whether the GCC's AI datacenter buildout resumes its pre-war trajectory or enters a prolonged pause:

1. **Microsoft Saudi Arabia launch (Q4 2026)**: Will it proceed on schedule or slip?
2. **HUMAIN and NEOM timelines**: The stc JV and DataVolt campus both have 2028 milestones — watch for delays
3. **Drone strike frequency**: Continued attacks on commercial infrastructure will deter further investment
4. **Sovereign fund calculus**: Will the PIF, QIA, and ADIA shift capital toward more stable geographies?
5. **US-China competition**: The geopolitical framing may push Washington to provide security guarantees for GCC AI infrastructure

The Gulf's bet on AI was always ambitious. Now it faces a test that no amount of sovereign wealth can fully insulate against: **the reality that compute infrastructure in a conflict zone is both a strategic asset and a strategic liability**.
