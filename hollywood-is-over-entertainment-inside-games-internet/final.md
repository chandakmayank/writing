# Hollywood Is Over. The Entertainment Business Is Inside Video Games Now.

Not a single one of the ten Best Picture nominees at the 2026 Oscars was filmed on a Hollywood soundstage.[^1] Some post-production happened in LA facilities. But every nominated movie — from *Marty Supreme* to *Sinners* to *Hamnet* — was shot somewhere else. New York. Louisiana. The UK.

Meanwhile, the former CEO of Sony Pictures visited his old stomping grounds and offered a bleak assessment: "The sunny version of Detroit. It was crickets. There's nothing going on."[^1]

Hollywood is dying. And the entertainment business — the real one, where the money and audience actually live — has already moved to video games and the internet.

---

## The Numbers Don't Lie

Los Angeles used to be the center of the entertainment universe. For a hundred years, talent, studios, infrastructure, and money clustered in a few square miles of Southern California. That cluster is disintegrating.

**Production in LA has collapsed.** Shoot days plunged from 36,792 in 2022 to just 19,694 in 2025 — a 46% drop in three years.[^1] Bloomberg reports shooting days are roughly 50% below their 2017 peak.[^2]

**The workers are leaving.** 41,000 entertainment workers fled Los Angeles from 2022 to 2024 — some by choice, many by necessity.[^1]

**Box office receipts are in free fall.** US and Canadian box office hit $8.6 billion in 2025, far below the nearly $12 billion peak in 2018.[^2] Inflation-adjusted receipts have plunged 40% since 2019, and audience numbers are cut in half.[^3]

**The jobs are dispersing.** LA's share of US film and TV employment dropped from 33% to 22% in just two years. The rest of the country's share rose from 54% to 69%.[^4] Production is fleeing to Canada, Georgia, anywhere with better tax incentives and lower costs.

**Oscars viewership keeps declining.** The 2026 ceremony drew 17.86 million viewers — down 9% from the previous year.[^5] The biggest night in Hollywood can't hold attention anymore.

The 2023 writers' and actors' strikes accelerated everything, shutting down scripted production for nearly five months.[^6] Studios responded with mass layoffs. Warner Bros Discovery got sold. Paramount promised $6 billion in "synergies" — which everyone understands means more cuts.[^1]

The most powerful person in entertainment isn't a studio boss. It's Ted Sarandos, co-CEO of Netflix — headquartered in Silicon Valley, not Hollywood.[^1]

---

## Gaming Is Bigger Than Movies. It's Not Even Close.

While Hollywood contracts, gaming has become the dominant form of entertainment on the planet.

**Gaming is the single largest sector of entertainment** — bigger than film and recorded music combined.[^7] The global gaming population hit 3.5 billion people by the end of 2025.[^8]

**The money is staggering:**
- The gaming creator economy generated **$10 billion** in 2025 across platform payouts, sponsorships, merchandise, subscriptions, and esports.[^9]
- YouTube and Twitch alone drive a **$9.3 billion** gaming content economy reaching 1.2 billion viewers.[^10]
- The live streaming market will hit **$62 billion** in 2026.[^11]

**Platform scale dwarfs traditional media:**
- Twitch: 31 million daily active viewers, 240 million monthly users[^9]
- Roblox: 85.3 million daily active users[^8]
- YouTube Gaming: 100 billion watch hours across 40 million active channels[^10]
- TikTok Live surpassed Twitch in total watch hours during Q1 2025 with 8 billion hours[^10]

And these aren't just people playing games. They're watching games. Living in game worlds. Building social lives around game platforms. The average gamer now spends **8.5 hours per week watching gaming content** — more time than many spend actually playing.[^10]

---

## Gaming Isn't Just Games Anymore. It's the New Social Infrastructure.

Fortnite started as a battle royale shooter. Now it's a platform where 12 million people showed up simultaneously to watch Travis Scott perform a virtual concert.[^12] Where Marvel movies get promoted through in-game events. Where the line between "game" and "social space" has essentially disappeared.

Roblox is even more extreme. It's a platform where users build their own games and worlds — and the top experiences have racked up over **60 billion visits**.[^7] "Adopt Me!" alone has produced more engagement than most Hollywood franchises could dream of. Roblox added Shopify integrations and virtual avatars, turning gaming into commerce.[^8]

These platforms are **digital venues** — not games with social features bolted on, but social spaces that happen to use game mechanics. Epic Games is designing Unreal Engine 6 as a metaverse development platform, explicitly aiming for interoperability between Fortnite, Minecraft, and Roblox.[^12]

The entertainment business didn't just move online. It moved into game worlds that are more engaging, more interactive, and more sticky than anything a movie theater can offer.

---

## Gen Z Has Already Left the Building

The generation that will define the next two decades of entertainment consumption has made its choice. And it's not movies.

**63% of Gen Z prefer playing video games to watching films.**[^8] 71% say gaming is a great way to socialize.[^8] 87% play video games at least weekly.[^13]

**Gaming takes 25% of Gen Z leisure time** — more than social media (18%) and streaming video (17%).[^8] The average Gen Z gamer plays **6 hours and 10 minutes per day.**[^8]

**They're mobile-first.** 86% of Gen Z identify as "mobile gamers first" (compared to just 29% of Gen X).[^8] 69% game on mobile, 42% on PC, 38% on console. Mobile esports viewership has surged 340% since 2022.[^8]

**They discover content through social platforms, not traditional media.** 73% of Gen Z prefer social media recommendations over broader platforms.[^14] They're finding what to watch and play through YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram — not through critics, trailers, or TV spots.

This generation isn't rejecting Hollywood. They're not angry at it. They just don't notice it. Their entertainment lives are built around interactive experiences, creator content, and social gaming platforms. The Oscars might as well be a rotary phone.

---

## The Reverse IP Pipeline: Hollywood Now Depends on Gaming

Here's the irony: while Hollywood declines, it's increasingly dependent on video games for the one thing it can't create anymore — fresh, culturally relevant IP.

**The Super Mario Bros. Movie grossed over $1 billion worldwide.**[^7] **The Last of Us** became HBO's flagship tentpole alongside *House of the Dragon*.[^7] Half of the top 10 most successful video game film adaptations were released within the last five years.[^7]

**50+ video game movies are currently in development.**[^15] The pipeline from game to screen has reversed: gaming is now the IP factory, and Hollywood is the distributor.

This makes sense. The most popular games are cultural artifacts with massive built-in audiences. Gamers spend hundreds of hours inside game worlds, developing deep empathy for characters and passion for stories. That kind of engagement — built through interaction, not passive viewing — is impossible to manufacture in a two-hour film.

Even platforms like Roblox represent IP opportunities. "Adopt Me!" has more visits than most film franchises have ticket sales. Gaming generates cultural gravity; Hollywood tries to capture the overflow.

Square Enix announced it would "aggressively pursue a multiplatform strategy" — moving away from PlayStation exclusivity to reach audiences everywhere.[^16] The game industry is platform-agnostic. Hollywood is still fighting over which theater gets the opening weekend.

---

## The Streaming Revolution Was Just the Beginning

Streaming disrupted traditional TV. But that was phase one. Phase two is the complete redefinition of what "entertainment" means.

**Live streaming is a $62 billion market.**[^11] Twitch, YouTube, and newcomers like Kick (offering a 95/5 revenue split to creators) are fighting for dominance. TikTok Live has emerged as a major player, surpassing Twitch in total hours watched.[^10]

**The creator economy has 50+ million independent content creators** building businesses directly with their audiences.[^17] Gaming is the single largest creator niche by revenue.[^9] Non-endemic brands — fast food chains, insurance companies, airlines — are moving ad budgets to gaming creators because that's where the engaged audience actually is.[^9]

**Streaming platforms are replacing traditional TV as the default medium.**[^18] People want control: on-demand, personalized, cross-device. They start a show on their TV, pause it, finish on their phone. Try doing that with broadcast television.

But here's what most analyses miss: **streaming was just the internet replacing cable.** The real disruption is interactive entertainment replacing passive viewing altogether. Why watch a story when you can live inside one?

---

## What This Means

The entertainment business of 2026 looks nothing like it did ten years ago. Here's what's actually happening:

**Power has shifted from institutions to platforms.** The studio system that controlled entertainment for a century is becoming a legacy content supplier. The real power lies with YouTube, Twitch, Roblox, TikTok — the platforms where audiences actually spend time.

**The creator has replaced the star.** A Twitch streamer with 31 million daily potential viewers has more cultural impact than most A-list actors. The $10 billion gaming creator economy proves that entertainment is now a direct relationship between creator and audience, with no studio middleman.

**Interactive has beaten passive.** Movies and TV are one-way experiences. Games, live streams, and virtual worlds are participatory. For a generation raised on interaction, passive entertainment feels like a relic.

**Gaming is the new default entertainment.** Not a hobby. Not a niche. The primary form of entertainment for the generation that will spend the most money over the next 40 years.

---

## The Old Gatekeepers Are Gone

Hollywood isn't dying because of bad movies or "woke" politics or streaming competition. It's dying because the fundamental model — centralized production, passive consumption, institutional gatekeeping — has been replaced by something more engaging, more accessible, and more economically efficient.

3.5 billion people play video games.[^8] 31 million watch Twitch daily.[^9] 85 million log into Roblox every day.[^8] These numbers make Hollywood's 17 million Oscar viewers look quaint.

The entertainment business didn't move to a new address. It dissolved into the internet. It lives inside game worlds, on live streams, in creator communities, and across platforms that didn't exist a decade ago.

Hollywood had a hundred-year run. It was spectacular while it lasted. But the audience has moved on — into worlds they can actually participate in, built by creators they actually follow, on platforms they actually control.

The entertainment business is alive and thriving. It just doesn't live in Hollywood anymore.
