# How AI Will Usher in the Age of Abundance and Universal High Income

## The Deflationary Revolution

"It's going to be massively deflationary."

That's Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, describing what artificial intelligence will do to the global economy. Not slightly deflationary. Not marginally cheaper. *Massively* deflationary — a force that could invert the fundamental assumption underlying every economic system in human history: that prices go up over time.[^1]

Think about what that means. For millennia, scarcity has been the organizing principle of civilization. Wars were fought over salt, spices, and oil. Empires rose and fell based on access to fertile land and trade routes. The entire discipline of economics — from Adam Smith to Milton Friedman — begins with one premise: resources are limited, and humans must compete for them.

What if that premise is about to become obsolete?

A growing chorus of technologists, economists, and AI researchers believe we are standing at the threshold of something unprecedented: an **age of abundance**, where artificial intelligence and robotics produce goods and services at near-zero marginal cost, where the price of intelligence itself collapses, and where "universal high income" — not just basic income, but genuine prosperity for all — becomes not just possible but inevitable.[^2]

The question isn't whether this is technically feasible. The economic data increasingly suggests it is. The question is whether we'll distribute the gains broadly enough to matter — or whether they'll accumulate in the hands of the few who control the machines.

---

## The Abundance Thesis

Let's define our terms. **Universal Basic Income (UBI)** is a policy that gives every citizen a regular cash payment regardless of employment status — a floor below which no one falls. **Universal High Income (UHI)** is something far more ambitious: a world where AI-driven productivity is so immense, and the cost of goods and services so low, that even modest income redistribution provides genuine prosperity.[^3]

The distinction matters. As Altman argued in late 2025, traditional UBI would "leave people wanting." He prefers the phrase "universal extreme wealth" — not a survival floor, but a genuine abundance where people can pursue meaningful lives without financial anxiety.[^4]

This isn't idle speculation from a single CEO. The vision is shared across Silicon Valley's most influential figures:

- **Dario Amodei**, CEO of Anthropic, dedicated his landmark essay "Machines of Loving Grace" to sketching what a world with powerful AI might look like "if everything goes right." He argues that most people are underestimating both the risks *and* the upside of AI — and that the upside is staggering.[^5]

- **Peter Diamandis**, founder of the XPRIZE and Singularity University, has developed the **MOSAIC model** — a concrete policy framework for funding universal high income through AI-driven economic gains.[^2]

- **Eric Schmidt**, former Google CEO, declared in a 2025 TED Talk that AI is "wildly under-hyped," with the most "extraordinary" benefits still ahead.[^6]

- **Elon Musk** has repeatedly stated that AI will create a future of abundance where work becomes optional.

The core claim is this: **AI and robotics are driving the marginal cost of production toward zero.** When the cost of intelligence — the ability to analyze, create, decide, and act — approaches zero, the cost of everything that intelligence produces follows it down. Healthcare, education, legal advice, software, manufacturing, energy — all become dramatically cheaper. The problem shifts from *production* (how do we make enough?) to *distribution* (how do we share what we can now produce in abundance?).[^2]

## The Economic Engine: Productivity at Scale

The skeptics have a fair point: Silicon Valley CEOs are notorious for overpromising. But the economic projections behind AI abundance aren't coming from tech hype machines alone. They're coming from the world's most respected economic research institutions.

**Goldman Sachs Research** projects that generative AI alone could raise global GDP by 7% — nearly **$7 trillion** — and lift productivity growth by 1.5 percentage points over a 10-year period. For context, U.S. productivity growth averaged just 1.1% annually from 2000 to 2019. AI could more than double that rate.[^7]

**PwC's 2025 "Value in Motion" report** is even more bullish: AI has the potential to boost global economic output by up to **15 percentage points** over the next decade. That's effectively adding one full percentage point to annual growth rates — comparable to the growth increment the world gained from 19th-century industrialization.[^8]

**McKinsey** projects that generative AI alone could boost annual productivity growth by 0.1 to 0.6 percentage points through 2040. Combined with other automation technologies, total productivity growth could see a 0.5 to 3.4 percentage point annual uplift — an economic step-change.[^9]

The MIT economist **Daron Acemoglu** offers a more conservative view, projecting just 1% GDP growth over 10 years.[^10] Even his pessimistic estimate, however, represents hundreds of billions in additional economic output. And Goldman Sachs expects AI to begin measurably boosting U.S. GDP as early as 2027.[^11]

### The Cost Collapse

Perhaps the most dramatic data point is the **exponential decline in AI inference costs** — the price of actually *running* AI models. According to **Epoch AI**, the cost to achieve GPT-4-level performance on PhD-level science questions has fallen by **40x per year**. Across six benchmarks they studied, price declines ranged from 9x to 900x per year.[^12]

This is not a gradual decline. It's a freefall. And it mirrors what happened with electricity, computing, and the internet — general-purpose technologies whose costs collapsed so rapidly that entirely new economies formed around them.

### The Productivity Gains Are Already Here

In software development — the sector where AI tools are most mature — the numbers are striking:

- **84% of developers** now use AI tools, and AI generates **41% of all new code** (2026)[^13]
- Developers report productivity gains of **25-39%** when using AI tools[^13]
- Controlled trials show AI coding tools cut task completion time by **50%**[^14]
- AI customer support assistants raised agent productivity by **14%**[^14]

These aren't projections. They're measured results happening right now. And they're concentrated in just one sector. As AI capabilities expand to cover biology, manufacturing, legal work, and education, the productivity gains will compound across the entire economy.

## Where Abundance Materializes

### Healthcare: 100 Years of Progress in a Decade

Dario Amodei, who has professional experience in both biology and neuroscience, argues that AI could compress "50-100 years of biological progress into 5-10 years."[^5]

The implications are staggering:

- **Drug discovery**: AI can design and simulate drug candidates orders of magnitude faster than traditional methods. What currently takes 10-15 years and $2 billion could be compressed to months.
- **Diagnostics**: AI systems are already matching or exceeding radiologist accuracy in detecting cancers. Soon, expert-level diagnosis will be available to every human on Earth, not just those near major hospitals.
- **Personalized medicine**: AI can analyze your genome, microbiome, and health history to create custom treatment plans — something no human doctor could do at scale.
- **Mental health**: AI therapists and coaches could provide 24/7 support, dramatically expanding access to mental healthcare.

Amodei's framework is useful here. He distinguishes between factors that *limit* intelligence's impact — the speed of biological experiments, the need for data, physical laws — and those that intelligence can eventually route around. In the short run, AI is bottlenecked by the physical world. In the long run, it finds ways through.[^5]

### Education: Expert Tutors for Every Human

Imagine a world where every child — regardless of income, geography, or language — has access to a personal tutor as skilled as the best teachers in human history. A tutor that never gets tired, never loses patience, and can adapt its teaching style in real-time to how each student learns best.

That's what AI-powered education looks like. The cost of expert instruction approaches zero. Language barriers disappear through real-time AI translation. The gap between a child in rural India and a child at a top private school narrows dramatically.

### Software and Knowledge Work

The most immediate impact is in software, where AI has already transformed development. But the trend extends to all knowledge work:

- Legal advice becomes accessible to everyone, not just those who can afford $500/hour attorneys
- Financial planning and tax optimization become available to all income levels
- Any person can build software applications by describing what they want to an AI
- Research, analysis, and writing — the work of consultants, analysts, and writers — becomes dramatically faster and cheaper

### Energy and Manufacturing

AI-optimized energy grids reduce waste. AI-accelerated materials science could crack fusion energy. AI-designed factories run by robots produce goods at a fraction of today's costs. The convergence of AI and robotics — what some call the "exponential replacement curve" — is already visible as Tesla, Unitree, and other manufacturers deploy robots in production facilities.[^15]

## From Abundance to Universal High Income

Here's the critical insight: **abundance alone doesn't guarantee prosperity for all.** If AI makes everything cheaper but all the savings flow to shareholders and platform owners, we get cheaper yachts for billionaires — not universal high income.

The automation paradox is real. Goldman Sachs estimates that AI could expose the equivalent of **300 million full-time jobs** to automation. Two-thirds of U.S. occupations are exposed to some degree, with 25-50% of workload in those roles potentially replaced.[^7] GDP can rise while human labor demand falls — and if nothing else changes, the gains accumulate where ownership and control sit.

### The MOSAIC Model

Peter Diamandis and Daniel Schreiber (CEO of Lemonade) have proposed a concrete framework called **MOSAIC** for funding universal high income:[^2]

1. **Dynamic VAT**: Capture part of AI-driven cost reductions by adjusting value-added taxes so that when AI makes things cheaper, the price gap funds UHI payments
2. **Ring-fenced AI profits**: Set aside above-trend tax receipts from AI-boosted corporate profits and capital gains
3. **Government automation savings**: Redirect the savings from automating government operations itself
4. **Consolidated welfare**: Merge fragmented welfare programs into one negative-income-tax-style payment, ensuring work always pays
5. **Public dashboard**: A transparent "AI dividend dashboard" showing citizens where the money comes from and where it goes

The message is pragmatic: the surplus *will* exist. The challenge is designing the plumbing to recycle it back to citizens early enough, before economic power fully consolidates.[^2]

### The Deflation Dividend

There's a simpler mechanism at work too. If AI makes goods and services radically cheaper — as Altman predicts — then even modest income redistribution goes much further. A $1,000 monthly payment in a world where healthcare costs $10/month, education is free, and housing is built by robots is very different from the same $1,000 in today's inflated economy.

Altman has argued that by the end of 2025, an individual spending $1,000 on AI inference could complete software projects that would previously have required an entire team working for months.[^1] If that trajectory continues, the cost of creating *anything* in the digital economy approaches zero — and the deflation dividend makes universal high income not just possible but almost unavoidable.

## The Critics: Why Abundance Isn't Guaranteed

For every optimist predicting abundance, there's a skeptic warning of techno-feudalism. The critics deserve a hearing.

### The Wage Compression Reality

Economist **Dean Baker** points to troubling data: labor compensation as a share of consumer spending has dipped below 72%, down from 75-76% before the pandemic. That gap represents about **$1 trillion in annual consumption** happening without wage growth to support it. Americans are buying stuff their paychecks can't justify, propped up by inflated stock portfolios and AI venture capital spending.[^16]

Baker argues that when the AI bubble bursts — and he believes it will — the crash could actually be "phenomenal for working class Americans." The bubble is distorting the economy, not improving it.[^16]

### Techno-Feudalism

Greek economist **Yanis Varoufakis** warns that we're not moving toward abundance — we're moving toward **techno-feudalism**. In his framework, profit drove capitalism, rent drove feudalism, and now "cloud capital" and "algorithmic capital" are creating a new system where value flows as rent to those who own the digital infrastructure.[^8]

The concern is concrete: if AI is deployed inside concentrated, lock-in heavy platforms, productivity gains won't show up as falling prices for consumers. They'll show up as higher rents, more subscriptions, more tolls and fees, and degraded quality hidden behind the same nominal price. This is what Cory Doctorow calls "enshittification" — platforms serve users well, then shift value to business customers, then extract everything for shareholders.[^2]

### The Productivity Paradox

Surveys show the number of people actually using AI at work is *falling* in some sectors. Many workers argue AI is "essentially useless to them" despite their employers' insistence it's revolutionary. MIT's Daron Acemoglu projects AI will add just 1% to GDP over a decade — far less than the optimists claim.[^10]

And then there are the infrastructure constraints. OpenAI's data centers require direct access to nuclear power because the national grid can't handle the compute demand. The company reportedly loses $3 for every $1 it earns.[^17] The trillion-dollar AI infrastructure buildout may face physical limits that even intelligence can't route around quickly.

### The Honest Assessment

Here's what the data actually supports:

- AI **will** dramatically increase productivity in many sectors
- AI costs **are** falling exponentially
- But productivity gains **don't automatically** become abundance for all
- Without deliberate distribution mechanisms, gains **will** concentrate at the top
- The timeline **is** uncertain — optimists say 2026-2030, skeptics say decades or never

Abundance is possible. It is not inevitable. The outcome depends on policy choices, ownership structures, and whether societies demand that the gains be shared.

## The Fundamental Bet

Dario Amodei frames the core concept well: in the AI age, we should be talking about **"the marginal returns to intelligence"** — figuring out what other factors become limiting when intelligence is very high.[^5]

The bet is this: intelligence is becoming the dominant factor of production, the way land was dominant in the agrarian era and capital was dominant in the industrial era. When you can run millions of AI instances at 10-100x human speed, the bottleneck shifts from *what can we figure out?* to *how fast can the physical world respond?*

If that bet is right — if intelligence becomes abundant and cheap — then the cost of everything intelligence produces must fall. Healthcare, education, software, legal advice, financial planning, research, design, manufacturing. The deflation Altman predicts isn't a bug. It's the natural consequence of intelligence becoming a commodity.

The question we face isn't technical. It's political and moral: **Will we distribute the gains?**

Amodei ends his essay with a call for both vigilance and hope: "Fear is one kind of motivator, but it's not enough: we need hope as well."[^5] The technology for abundance is arriving. The economics support it. The policy frameworks exist. What remains is the will to build a system where the age of AI abundance lifts all boats — not just the yachts.
