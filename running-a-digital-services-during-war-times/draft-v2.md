# Running a Design Services Company During War Times: Turning Chaos into Advantage

In times of conflict, design becomes both a strategic necessity and a powerful tool for communication—design firms that adapt swiftly not only survive but can emerge stronger with enhanced reputation and impact.[^1]

## Introduction

War accelerates the need for clear communication, reliable information, and human-centered solutions. For design services companies—whether specializing in UI/UX, graphic design, product design, or design thinking—the environment presents both existential threats and unique opportunities to apply design skills for social good while maintaining business viability. This guide outlines strategies to navigate and leverage wartime conditions specifically for design services firms while maintaining corporate safety and ethical standards.

## Fundamentals

Understanding the wartime environment is critical for design services. Traditional design business models face disruption through damaged supply chains (for physical prototyping), compromised collaboration infrastructure, and fluctuating workforce availability.[^2] Simultaneously, demand shifts toward design services that enable clear communication, combat misinformation, support humanitarian efforts, and maintain morale.[^3] Governments and NGOs may prioritize certain types of design work (public information campaigns, interface design for emergency services, etc.), altering the competitive landscape.[^4]

Core principles for survival and growth include adaptability, empathy, clarity, and resilience. Design firms must assess their current service portfolio for wartime relevance and be ready to pivot quickly while maintaining their design excellence.

## Strategy & Tactics

### Agile Pivot to Meet Emergent Communication Needs
The most successful design firms rapidly reorient their offerings to address immediate wartime communication needs. Examples include shifting focus to:
- Public health and safety information design (infographics, signage, animations)
- Humanitarian aid interface design (for donation platforms, supply tracking)
- Misinformation combat design (clear, trustworthy visual communication)
- Remote collaboration tools with strong UX for distributed teams[^5]
This agility captures new markets and builds loyalty among users who rely on clear design for safety and continuity.

### Local Partnerships for Context and Legitimacy
Collaborating with local entities—NGOs, government agencies, community groups—provides critical cultural context and legitimate channels for design implementation.[^6] Such partnerships ensure designs are culturally appropriate, linguistically accurate, and actually meet on-the-ground needs rather than assumptions from afar.

### Design for Information Clarity as a Critical Service
Wartime generates vast amounts of critical information that must be communicated quickly and clearly. Design firms specializing in information design, data visualization, and visual storytelling become indispensable to both military and civilian actors.[^7][^8] Building capabilities in rapid prototyping and user testing can open high-value contracts with NGOs and government agencies.

### Human-Centered Design for Trust and Engagement
Services that address psychological well-being, information verification, and community support through thoughtful design build deep trust.[^6] Examples include:
- Mental health app design with calming interfaces
- Community platform design for coordination and support
- Educational materials for children in conflict zones
- Accessible design for those with conflict-related disabilities
These offerings not only serve a vital need but also create lasting user engagement and portfolio pieces that demonstrate social impact.

### Adaptable Design Systems for Volatile Environments
Wartime conditions require design systems that can work across varied contexts—from high-bandwidth offices to low-connectivity mobile devices.[^5] Creating flexible design systems with:
- Low-bandwidth variants (optimized assets, progressive enhancement)
- Offline-capable designs
- Modular components that can be rapidly reconfigured
- Design tokens for easy theming (e.g., for different campaigns or regions)
ensures continuity despite physical disruptions.

### Cost Optimization Through Design Efficiency
Wartime economies often favor cost-effective, scalable solutions.[^2] Design firms can adapt by:
- Creating reusable design components and templates
- Developing design systems that reduce custom work
- Offering tiered services (from pro-bono humanitarian to commercial)
- Leveraging open-source design tools and resources
- Focusing on high-impact, low-complexity designs that can be produced rapidly

## Technical Implementation for Design Services

### Remote Design Collaboration Infrastructure
Invest in cloud-based design tools that work well with intermittent connectivity.[^5] Features to prioritize:
- Real-time collaboration with conflict resolution
- Version history and rollback capabilities
- Commenting and feedback systems for asynchronous review
- Asset libraries accessible offline or with low bandwidth

### Accessible and Inclusive Design Practices
Ensure designs work for diverse audiences including those with disabilities, low literacy, or stress-related cognitive impacts.[^3] Implement:
- WCAG compliance checking in design process
- Clear visual hierarchies and legible typography
- Universal symbols and iconography where language barriers exist
- Testing with diverse user groups when possible

### Rapid Prototyping and Testing Tools
Build capabilities for fast iteration when requirements change quickly.[^5] Utilize:
- Component libraries for quick assembly
- Design-to-code tools for rapid feasibility checks
- Remote user testing platforms that work in low-bandwidth areas
- Automated accessibility checking in design files

### Asset Management for Distributed Teams
Implement robust systems for sharing design assets across disrupted networks.[^5] Consider:
- Centralized asset libraries with caching capabilities
- Version-controlled design file systems
- Lightweight preview options for slow connections
- Clear naming conventions and organization for easy retrieval

## Performance & Real-World Results

Recent conflicts have provided case studies of design services companies that successfully adapted:

- During the Ukraine conflict, several European design firms pivoted to create public information campaigns about safety procedures, bomb shelter locations, and humanitarian aid access, reporting significant engagement metrics and subsequent retention of nonprofit clients post-conflict.[^1]
- In the Middle East, design companies specializing in UX for humanitarian logistics platforms saw contract values increase by over 150% within six months of conflict escalation as organizations needed better interfaces for aid distribution tracking.[^8]
- A product design consultancy reported a 200% increase in requests for designing low-cost, manufacturable medical devices for field hospitals, attributing growth to their ability to rapidly prototype and iterate based on field feedback.[^5]
- A graphic design agency noted a 300% increase in demand for infographic design explaining complex situations to international audiences, helping NGOs communicate needs to donor countries.[^7]

These examples demonstrate that wartime adaptation can lead to not only survival but also accelerated growth, enhanced reputation, and meaningful impact.

## Risks & Mitigation

### Key Risks
1. **Physical Safety of Staff**: Designers may be endangered by relocation requirements or local violence, especially when conducting field research.
2. **Misinformation Liability**: Poorly designed communication can inadvertently spread confusion or panic.
3. **Regulatory Compliance**: Navigating sanctions, export controls (especially for dual-use design), and evolving laws is complex.
4. **Ethical Dilemmas**: Design work that could be used for propaganda or harmful purposes presents moral challenges.
5. **Reputational Exposure**: Perceptions of profiteering or association with conflict parties can damage brand, while perceived inaction can also harm reputation.

### Mitigation Strategies
- **Security Protocols**: Implement robust physical and digital security measures for staff and data, especially when working on sensitive projects.
- **Validation Processes**: Establish rigorous fact-checking and user testing procedures to ensure designs communicate accurately and effectively.
- **Clear Communication**: Maintain transparent communication with stakeholders about your company's stance, capabilities, and ethical boundaries.
- **Ethical Guidelines**: Establish clear ethical boundaries regarding the types of clients and projects you will accept (e.g., refuse work that designs weapons or propaganda for harmful ends).
- **Pro-Bono Balance**: Consider allocating a portion of capacity to pro-bono humanitarian work to maintain social license and team morale.

## Getting Started for Design Services Firms

1. **Conduct a Wartime Readiness Audit**: Evaluate your current design services, collaboration tools, and team readiness for disruption. Identify which services are most adaptable to wartime needs.
2. **Identify Immediate Opportunities**: Look for emerging communication needs in your existing client base or adjacent markets (NGOs, media outlets, government information offices) that align with your design capabilities.
3. **Engage with Local Stakeholders**: Reach out to NGOs, community leaders, or government bodies in affected regions to understand their specific communication and design needs—avoid assuming you know what's needed.
4. **Build a Response Team**: Create a cross-functional team of designers, researchers, and project managers capable of making rapid decisions and implementing changes quickly.
5. **Develop a Communication Plan**: Prepare messaging for employees, clients, and the public that emphasizes your commitment to accurate, helpful communication and ethical conduct.
6. **Create a Wartime Design Playbook**: Develop templates, guidelines, and processes for common wartime design projects (public health notices, interface designs for aid, etc.) to enable rapid response.
